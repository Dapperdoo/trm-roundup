THE MORNING AFTER

Interlude Special
No football today. The managers are the entertainment.

There are no fixtures today. The World Cup has reached its final and is holding its breath. Which means we have nothing to report — no goals, no points, no drama — and so, in the great tradition of entertainers who've run out of material, we're turning the spotlight on the only people left to roast: the thirteen managers of this league. Pull up a chair. This is the bit where the football stops and the therapy begins.

Let's start at the top, because Joe A is sitting on 461 points and an eleven-point lead and he would very much like you to know that he has been working extremely hard to get there. Specifically, he has been working extremely hard trying to get AI tools to do what they promise. Every day: a new prompt, a new workflow, a new tab open, a new crushing disappointment. Joe A's relationship with artificial intelligence is essentially a long-distance romance where the other party keeps standing him up. He persists. He troubleshoots. He opens another Guinness and tries again. Somewhere in that beautiful chaos, he assembled a decent squad. Whether the lead holds is another matter — Tristan is breathing down his neck at third, and Tristan does not look like a man who is going to quietly accept second place. Tristan looks like a man who is about to flip a table.

Tristan, for those unfamiliar, is the big Russian Londoner who runs his entire operation by hand — every figure pulled manually from the FIFA website, cross-referenced against Jez's spreadsheet, which Jez built from scratch with his bare brain and which Tristan has since adopted as holy scripture. Jez, meanwhile, is sitting fifth on 360 points, quietly unimpressed by everything. He could have built an algorithm. He chose not to. He picks on instinct, taste, and the accumulated wisdom of a man who has seen too much to be impressed by a chatbot. He watches the rest of the league reaching for their tech solutions and he says nothing. He just makes a note. In a spreadsheet. That he built himself. By hand.

Speaking of tech solutions — Chris. Lloydy. The mad scientist of the group, currently ninth on 320 points, who entered the transfer window with a self-built AI algorithm and emerged having submitted five consecutive bids for players from the 2022 World Cup. Not 2026. 2022. His algorithm wasn't scouting the tournament; it was holding a séance. It reached through time, rattled its chains, and tried to sign men who have since retired, aged out, or been eliminated so long ago they've had time to write their memoirs. One valid bid eventually squeaked through and scored 8 points, which is the equivalent of a stopped clock being right once and demanding a column in The Times. Jake, who has been warning everyone about Lloydy's AI activities for months — the algorithm, the office complex, the private experiments he is absolutely definitely conducting in there — received the news of the malfunction with the calm, measured grace of a man who has been completely vindicated. Which is to say he was insufferable about it.

Jake. Snacob's Ladder. Currently twelfth on 292 points, which is not where he wanted to be, but he is a man with priorities. He loves his dentist. He will tell you about his dentist. He has been to the dentist more than most people have been to the shops, and he is enthusiastic about every visit in a way that suggests either very good dental care or a deeply unusual relationship with a reclining chair and a bright light. He also injured himself on the way to the dentist — not at the dentist, not leaving the dentist, but in transit, en route, before anything had even happened — which is exactly the kind of injury record that puts him in the same bracket as Joe S. Joe S, the ex-pro, the genuinely gifted footballer whose career spent more time in the physio room than on the pitch, who is currently sixth on 338 points and still has the touch of a man who could have been brilliant if his body had ever agreed to cooperate. Between Jake and Joe S, this league has produced two men for whom the human skeleton is essentially a suggestion.

Back to Jake briefly, because he is still furious about the transfers. A couple of days ago, Jeremy quietly corrected an honest mistake — swapped out an injured player Jake had bought, replaced him with a fit one, everyone nodded, everyone moved on. Everyone except Jake, who has filed it away next to the Lloydy AI situation and the dentist incident as evidence that the universe is specifically organised against him. 'SNACOB'S LADDER!' he shouts, to no one in particular, reminding himself and the room that he exists and his team exists and justice has not yet been served. The drinks tray wobbles. Someone nearby gets wet.

Sam is fourth on 361 points and is probably the most naturally gifted person in this league at absolutely everything except, possibly, property research. Sam once signed a year-long lease on a house because the viewing had its own bar. He moved in. There was no bathroom. He stood in his new home, surrounded by empty barrels, and contemplated his choices. His brother Wigs — tenth, 319 points, the counsellor of the group — has heard this story many times and responds to it with the particular patience of a man who has spent his professional life listening to people work through their decisions. Wigs is gentle, gregarious, and constitutionally incapable of being unkind, which makes him the perfect foil for a league that is mostly unkind about everything. The brothers are separated by six places in the table. Cricket season cannot come soon enough for either of them.

Dan is seventh on 329 points, living off-grid, making smart moves, saying witty things to no one in particular because there is no one nearby to hear them. His ally Malik is thirteenth — last — on 280 points, managing from Iceland, signing anyone who has ever pulled on a Manchester United or Portugal shirt. Portugal, it should be noted, have been eliminated. Their entire squad is gone. Malik's transfer philosophy has just lost its southern European wing entirely, leaving him with the Manchester United half of the operation, which is its own kind of adventure. Dave is eleventh on 300 points, composing a poem about it, saving the cymbals for a special occasion, and quietly drafting a pitch for a Culture Show segment in which he explains, at length, why eleventh place is actually a rich and complex cultural position that rewards close reading. Tom is eighth on 321 points and is almost certainly fine. A source close to the situation reports that he is 'very happy' and 'somewhere'. The source could not be more specific. Nick is second on 370 points, which is remarkable for a man whose natural instinct on a football pitch is to launch himself, studs-up, at the nearest shin and whose shots routinely clear the crossbar by the width of a postcode.

So that's the room. Thirteen managers, one trophy, and a final still to be played. The football returns soon — and when it does, the points will matter again, the spreadsheet will update, the algorithm will attempt to bid for someone who retired in 2019, and Jake will shout his team name into the void. We'll be ready.

The World Cup Final is coming. One match. One winner. Thirteen managers watching with everything to play for — and at least one of them watching from a location that has not yet been confirmed. The Morning After will be back when the football is.
