THE MORNING AFTER

Interlude Special
No football today. You're all getting it instead.

Good morning. There are no fixtures today. The World Cup pauses for breath at the semi-final stage, and since there are no goals to report, no blanks to mock and no hauls to celebrate, we are turning the spotlight — slowly, deliberately, with great affection — onto the thirteen people who have been making our lives interesting since this tournament began. Settle in. This is going to take a moment.

Let's start at the top, because Joe A is sitting there at 415 points and honestly seems slightly surprised about it himself. Shatner's Bassoon leads the league, which is remarkable chiefly because Joe spends most of his waking hours trying to get AI tools to do what they claim they can do — a crusade that has, by all accounts, never once fully succeeded. He is essentially a man who keeps ordering the same dish, getting sent something completely different, eating it anyway, and then logging back on to try again. Tristan, his great rival, is third on 358 points and absolutely seething about it. Tristan gleans every single number by hand from the FIFA website — manually, painstakingly, with his enormous hands — and he is still behind a man whose main hobby is arguing with chatbots. He has been seen throwing his hands up in disgust at least four times this week, and the tournament hasn't even finished.

Nick is second on 358 — level with Tristan on points but ranked above him, which will be doing wonders for Tom's blood pressure. Tom is seventh, 51 points back, and Tom and Nick have been circling each other like rival stags since matchday one. The thing about Nick is that he is very tall, very loud, and on a football pitch he is a reckless, clumsy, joyfully dangerous menace who flies into tackles with the energy of a man who has absolutely nothing to lose and no particular interest in the ball. His shooting, meanwhile, goes approximately forty feet over the crossbar every single time, like a man attempting to chip a golf ball over a mountain. His fantasy management, to be fair, is considerably more accurate. Tom would like you to know he is not bothered. Tom is very bothered.

Sam is fourth on 347 points, which is an excellent return for a man who loves Shakespeare more than football, cricket more than either, and beer more than all three combined. His team is called 'Look at his face. Just Look at his FACE!' and it remains, across the entire history of this league, the single most accurate team name ever registered. His brother Wigs is tenth on 294 — the two of them separated by three managers and about fifty points, which is the kind of sibling rivalry that makes Christmas dinner interesting. Wigs is a counsellor by profession, which means he is professionally equipped to process the emotional damage of finishing below his brother. Whether he is actually doing that is another matter entirely.

Jeremy is fifth on 346 points, which feels about right for a man who built the spreadsheet this entire league runs off, picks his team on pure instinct and taste, and watches everyone else's algorithmic experiments with the quiet, withering patience of a man who has seen this film before and knows exactly how it ends. He does not use a system. He does not need one. He simply knows. Which brings us, inevitably, to Chris — Lloydy — ninth on 297, whose algorithm did not know. Whose algorithm, in fact, appeared to believe it was 2022, firing off five consecutive bids for players from the previous World Cup before finally, mercifully, landing on someone valid. Someone who did at least score 8 points, so the machine redeemed itself slightly, the way a sat-nav redeems itself by eventually getting you to the right country. Jeremy has not said a word about it publicly. He doesn't need to. The silence is doing all the heavy lifting.

Jake, eleventh on 289 points, has plenty to say about it, however. Jake has plenty to say about everything. He is a renewable-energy project manager who loves wind turbines, mushrooms, and — with a passion that borders on the spiritual — his dentist. He injured himself getting to the dentist once. Not at the dentist. On the way there. This is the kind of man we are dealing with. He is currently carrying two grudges: one against Chris, whose AI activities Jake monitors with the vigilance of a man who genuinely believes something is being cooked up in Lloydy's office complex that the rest of us aren't cleared to know about; and one against Jeremy, who corrected an honest transfer mistake a couple of days ago — swapping out an injured player Jake had bought for an uninjured one — to the quiet relief of every other manager in the league, and to the absolute, unrelenting fury of Jake alone. Every other manager moved on. Jake has not moved on. Jake will not move on. Somewhere, right now, Jake is shouting 'SNACOB'S LADDER!' into the middle distance and thinking about it.

Dan is eighth on 305 — smart, witty, living off-grid, historically one of the best fantasy managers in this league, and allied with Malik, who is thirteenth and last on 278. Malik is managing from Iceland, which is either a disadvantage or a superpower depending on how you look at it, and his transfer policy is elegantly simple: if a player has ever worn a Manchester United or Portugal shirt, Malik wants him. Portugal, of course, have been eliminated. Malik has been informed of this. He is processing it in his own time.

Dave is twelfth on 280, and Dave is a poet and lecturer who loves wordplay, city breaks, beer, cigarettes, and the dream — still alive, still burning — of one day hosting his own segment on The Culture Show. He has the segment name ready. He has had it ready for years. 'Trossy's Wafflecock', in which he would hold forth on cultural nothingness with such earnest intellectual conviction that viewers at home would briefly wonder if it meant something before concluding, warmly, that it absolutely did not. Dave, we believe in you. We believe in the segment. We believe in the cymbal.

And finally — because we must — Joe S. Sixth place, 326 points, Back of the Van United. A man of genuine, rare footballing talent who spent roughly half his playing career on a treatment table rather than a pitch, being told by physios that whatever he'd done to himself this time was, medically speaking, quite impressive. He is beloved by everyone in this league, baffled by most technology, and has lived a life that could fill several books — none of which he would write himself because he'd probably injure his hand opening the laptop. We are glad he is here. We are glad he is upright.

The semi-finals are coming. Four nations left standing, and somewhere in this league, thirteen managers are doing their maths, checking their squads, and quietly hoping the football does the talking for them. It will. It always does. But today was ours.

The next fixtures will be the semi-finals, and when they arrive, The Morning After will be back with the numbers, the hauls, the blanks, and the full, unsparing reckoning. Until then — look after yourselves. Especially Jake. Seriously, Jake, please just stay indoors.
