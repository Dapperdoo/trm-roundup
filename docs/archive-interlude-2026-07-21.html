THE MORNING AFTER

Interlude Special
No football. So let's talk about you lot.

There are no fixtures today. The World Cup Final is still to come, and between now and then the tournament is holding its breath. Which means, for the first time in weeks, we have a moment to put down the spreadsheet, step away from the data, and turn the spotlight where it arguably belongs — on the thirteen human beings who have been shouting at their phones, refreshing their squads and quietly losing their minds since this whole thing kicked off. Settle in. This is for you.

Let's start at the top, because Joe A is sitting on 461 points and a league lead that is, frankly, starting to feel like a personality. Shatner's Bassoon, first place. He'll tell you it's down to good signings and shrewd management. What he won't tell you is how many hours he spent this week trying to get an AI tool to do something — anything — it actually promised to do, only to watch it confidently produce the wrong answer, apologise, produce a different wrong answer, and apologise again. Joe A's relationship with artificial intelligence is essentially a long-distance relationship where only one party is aware it's happening. Meanwhile, Tristan is breathing down his neck in third, 97 points back, doing his data-gathering entirely by hand — sat there with the FIFA website open, entering numbers like a man who trusts no one and nothing, which, honestly, is the correct response to this league. He throws his hands up. He suffers no fools. He is also, quietly, coming for Joe A, and Joe A should probably look up from his broken chatbot long enough to notice.

Now, Nick is in second place, which is remarkable for a man whose entire footballing philosophy can be summarised as: hit it as hard as possible and see what happens. On the pitch, Nick tackles like a man who has never been introduced to the concept of 'the ball' and is simply making do. Off it, he is loud, enormous, and great fun, which is why everyone keeps inviting him to things despite the bruises. His shooting? A wild, soaring golf swing that clears the crossbar by a distance that suggests he is actually aiming for a different sport entirely. Second place, though. Genuinely second place. The man is doing something right, even if we can't work out what.

Over on the creative wing, we have Dave, our lecturer-poet, a man who has spent more time thinking about the cultural significance of a well-placed semicolon than most people spend thinking about anything. Dave is in eleventh, which he will process through the lens of art, loss, and the human condition, and then write something about it that is genuinely quite good. He still dreams of 'Trossy's Wafflecock' — his proposed Culture Show segment in which he would ramble, beautifully, about nothing in particular for seven minutes and somehow make you feel it meant everything. The cymbals, we are told, are reserved for special occasions. This is not one of them. Not yet. Wigs, meanwhile — Dave's equivalent on the counselling side of the arts — is in tenth, one point behind Chris, and is the kind of man who will listen patiently to your problems, reflect them back with warmth and insight, and then go and watch cricket. His brother Sam is up in fourth, which is either inspiring or infuriating depending on your relationship with your own siblings. Sam is an expressive, scatty, Shakespearean man who loves beer, dancing, and cricket in roughly equal measure, and whose life decisions are — let's say — colourful. He once signed a year-long lease on a house because the viewing had its own bar. Moved in. No bathroom. None. The bar, at least, was fully stocked.

Then there is Joe S. Back of the Van United. Sixth place. A man of genuine, luminous footballing talent who spent approximately half his playing career on a treatment table being told to rest, which is the universe's cruellest joke given how much he clearly wanted to be on the pitch. The injuries were legendary. Hamstrings, knees, things that don't have names. He loves a party, hates a physio appointment, and is baffled by technology in a way that is both endearing and slightly alarming. Joe S once looked at a QR code like it had personally offended him. Sixth is respectable. He got there on instinct and charisma, which is how he did everything.

Speaking of instinct: Jeremy. The professor. Fifth place, 360 points, and the man who built the very spreadsheet this entire league runs on — a feat of programming elegance that Tristan still uses to this day, filling it in by hand like a monk illuminating a manuscript. Jeremy could, if he wanted, build an algorithm to pick his team. He has the skills. He simply chooses not to, because Jeremy picks on taste, experience, and the quiet confidence of someone who has been doing this longer than most people have been interested in football. He watches Lloydy's algorithmic adventures with the expression of a surgeon watching someone perform an operation with a spanner. Not angry. Just... tired.

Which brings us, inevitably, to Chris. Lloydy. Ninth place. The mad scientist. The man whose algorithm — his pride, his project, his baby — recently held the entire league's transfer window hostage while it fired off five consecutive bids for players from the 2022 World Cup. Players who, in some cases, have since retired. Players who are, at this point, exactly the sort of names you'd find on a commemorative sticker album. The algorithm was, in a very real sense, trying to sign ghosts. It did eventually land one valid bid, and that player scored 8 points, which Lloydy will mention until he dies. Jake, in twelfth, has been vindicated so completely by this episode that he can barely speak without grinning. 'SNACOB'S LADDER!' he shouts, to no one in particular, simply to remind himself he exists and his team is real and he is not, in fact, a figment of an algorithm's fever dream. Jake injured himself last week. Not at training. Not at five-a-side. On his way to the dentist — a journey he was, by all accounts, genuinely excited about, because Jake loves the dentist with a fervour that most people reserve for cup finals. He arrived limping, beaming, and was apparently delighted by the whole experience regardless. Harry Kane, meanwhile, continues to cost him money.

Down at the bottom, Malik is managing from Iceland with 280 points and a transfer policy that can be described simply as: Manchester United or Portugal, ideally both. Portugal, of course, have been eliminated. This has not, one suspects, shaken Malik's fundamental worldview. His ally Dan is in seventh, off-grid and unbothered, making smart picks in a shed somewhere while the rest of us panic-refresh. And Tom — eighth, 321 points — is Tom. Wherever Tom is right now, whatever he's doing, he is probably fine.

The World Cup Final is the last fixture standing — one match, winner takes all, and every remaining player either banks their last points or watches from the stands. For thirteen managers, that means one final swing at the table. Whatever happens, The Morning After will be there. It always is.
