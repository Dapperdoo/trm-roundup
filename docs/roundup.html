THE MORNING AFTER

Quarter-Final Eve Special · The Rest-Day Roast · No Football Was Harmed
Thursday 9 July · one microphone · thirteen defenceless managers

Thursday. No football until nine o'clock tonight — which means, for the first time in three weeks, there is absolutely nothing to report. No hauls, no heroes, no Haaland. Just thirteen grown men and a frozen league table. So pull up a chair and switch your phones off. Tonight's show isn't about the football. Tonight, it's about you.

Let's start at the top, because he'd want us to. Joe A — twenty-nine points clear and utterly insufferable about it. This man won the transfer window back in the group stage and has spent every day since doing a slow lap of honour round his own living room, refreshing the table to confirm he's still right. He files his tax return in April. He colour-codes his socks. Give him a day with no football and he does what he does best — precisely nothing, immaculately — radiating the serene smugness of a man who knows the rest of us can't reach him. Enjoy it, Joe. Hubris is a quarter-final tradition.

Twenty-nine points back, doing his finest impression of a tragic hero, is Sam — second place, a pint, and a running Shakespearean monologue nobody ordered. "To press the transfer button, or not to press." He spends his rest days willing Haaland to score in matches that aren't being played, the face doing all the heavy lifting: part hope, part heartburn. The eternal nearly-man — forever one Norwegian hat-trick from glory, and one heavy sigh from the sofa.

Now, if Sam's overthinking it, spare a thought for the two who've made overthinking a lifestyle. Jeremy — the professor — has spent the entire rest day rebuilding his model from first principles: a spreadsheet the size of a small moon, back-testing quarter-final permutations no living human requested. He'll surface tonight with forty tabs, three confidence intervals, and precisely the same gut feeling he started with. And in the next seat, Tristan, whose whole operation runs on a data feed he siphons off Jez's mother site — a feed that, on a day with no football, serves nothing but tumbleweed. He's spent the afternoon poking the relay to check it hasn't fallen asleep, which, given its track record, isn't paranoia. It's grief counselling for a server.

And speaking of hopelessly out of date — Chris. Somewhere in his corner an ancient algorithm still insists Brazil are 4/1 favourites and the USA have "real momentum," a machine so behind the times it qualifies for listed-building status. The beauty of Chris is that he ignores it completely and does fine anyway. Trust the vibe, not the silicon.

Speaking of grief — and we must, because there's an epidemic of it — this rest day is high season for one man. Wigs. The counsellor. His couch is stacked: Tom clutching a Colombia shirt, still sobbing over thirty-three points and an entire legion binned on a single penalty run-up — a man who can only really process loss with a fresh festival wristband and a warm cup raised to a field with questionable plumbing. And beside him, Malik, gazing into an Icelandic fjord, watching horizontal sleet, quietly asking why he runs a fantasy team from latitude 64 with better views than wifi. Wigs hands out the good two-ply, nods sympathetically, and sits a comfortable ninth needing counsel from precisely no one. Business is booming.

Then there's the danger list — men for whom a day off is a genuine health-and-safety hazard. Joe S, our beloved ex-pro, a career that read less like a highlight reel than a medical dossier, a man who can tweak a hamstring reaching for the remote — the medical guidance ahead of the quarters is simply: do not move. And Jake, fully capable of injuring himself celebrating a goal that happened on another continent, currently climbing the table and one over-enthusiastic fist-pump from the physio's table, with a dentist's appointment already looming on the calendar like a horror-film reveal. Between them, a safety seminar waiting to happen.

Which leaves the three who've simply opted out. Nick's at the driving range, alone, hosing 9-irons into a net, chasing a tempo he last owned in 2011 and insisting the new grip "feels great, actually." It doesn't. Dan's off-grid entirely — solar-powered, gloriously unreachable, strumming a bassline by candlelight, cheerfully unaware he's even in twelfth, which is frankly the healthiest relationship anyone here has with this competition. And propping up the whole edifice, dead last, Dave — who's taken a five-point week and a bottom-of-the-table finish and done the only thing a poet can: reached for the quill. 'Trossy's Wafflecock' is, as we speak, producing a fourteen-stanza epic on the cruelty of the penalty spot, metaphors stacked like unstable pancakes, a rhyme for "Cubarsí" being wrestled to the ground even now.

And that's your league — thirteen men, one frozen table, not a single ball kicked. Get it out of your systems: at nine o'clock tonight France play Morocco, the points come flooding back, and every last one of you scuttles straight back behind your footballers. Enjoy the last quiet night, gaffers. You've earned the ribbing. See you in the wreckage.
