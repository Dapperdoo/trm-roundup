THE MORNING AFTER

Interlude Special
No ball, no mercy — just thirteen managers in the dock

No football today. The tournament has reached its final breath — one match left, the whole thing balanced on a knife-edge — and rather than stare at a blank fixture list, we're turning the spotlight on the people who've been sweating, swearing and refreshing their phones for the past several weeks. Ladies and gentlemen: your thirteen managers. Please hold your applause. Some of them don't deserve it yet.

Let's start at the top, because Joe A is sitting on 461 points and a 91-point lead, and he would like you to know that he's very relaxed about it. Genuinely. Completely unbothered. Which is why he has spent the last fortnight trying to get four different AI tools to do one simple task — and failing, every single time. Joe's entire side-hustle is a doomed crusade to make technology keep its promises, and it never does, and he keeps going back. He is, in the most affectionate possible sense, the man who argues with the self-checkout machine and always loses. Tristan is closing, by the way. Ninety-one points sounds comfortable until you remember there's one game left and Tristan is the sort of man who throws his hands up in disgust at a dropped sweet, let alone a dropped league title.

Speaking of Tristan: big lad, sharp mind, bottomless bag of sweeties, and a fantasy operation run entirely by hand — every number pulled personally from the FIFA website, one laborious click at a time, cross-referenced against Jez's legendary spreadsheet. He does NOT have a shortcut. He does NOT have a system. He has patience, stubbornness, and a look he gives you when you've said something stupid that could strip paint at twenty metres. If Joe A does bottle this, Tristan will not say a word. He will simply look at him. That will be enough.

Now — Jeremy. Fifth place, 360 points, and still the man who built the spreadsheet that the entire league runs on. The professor. The one who could absolutely build an algorithm if he wanted to — and chooses not to, because he picks his team on instinct, taste and thirty years of knowing what good football looks like. He watches Lloydy's AI bid for players from 2022 with the expression of a man who has just found a child trying to rewire a plug with a crayon. Quietly appalled. Privately delighted. Says nothing. Needs to say nothing. Meanwhile, Chris — Lloydy — is down in ninth on 320 points, and his algorithm is somewhere in the lab trying to sign Luka Modrić again. The machine fired five bids for the 2022 squad before it fluked one valid transfer, which did at least score 8 points, so we'll call it one-for-six and move on. The mad scientist lives to experiment another day.

And then there's Jake. Twelfth on 292 points, still furious — and I mean still, as in two days ago — about Jeremy swapping out an injured player in the transfer window. Every other manager shrugged and said fair enough. Jake filed it away under 'unforgivable'. Jake also blew a significant chunk of his budget on Harry Kane, filled the rest of the squad with brave unknowns, and has spent the tournament loudly shouting 'SNACOB'S LADDER!' at irregular intervals to remind himself — and anyone within a 40-metre radius — who he is and what he stands for. He injured himself recently, which is worth noting mainly because the last time he did it he was on his way to the dentist. The dentist. He loves the dentist. He will tell you at length how much he loves the dentist. He also insists on dancing while holding a full tray of drinks, and if you are reading this from a dry-cleaning shop, Jake is probably why.

Over to the brothers. Sam is fourth — Look at his FACE! — and that face is currently doing something expressive and theatrical, because Sam is a stage and screen man who has never once had a neutral reaction to anything in his life. He loves beer, Shakespeare, cricket, dancing, and music, roughly in that order depending on the day. He once signed a year-long lease on a house because the viewing had its own bar, only to discover on moving day that there was no bathroom. No bathroom. He looked at the empty bar barrels. He made his calculations. We move on. His brother Wigs is tenth, which means Sam is currently beating him by 42 points, and if you think that hasn't come up at family dinner, you don't know this family.

Nick is second — second! — on 370 points, and credit where it's due, that is a serious haul. Nick is tall, loud, and on the football pitch an absolute menace: he flies into tackles like a man who has confused 'football match' with 'controlled demolition', frequently on purpose, leaving opponents in various states of distress. His shooting, meanwhile, goes high, wide, and somewhere over the crossbar into the next postcode. That he is second in this league is either a triumph of squad-building or proof that the universe rewards chaos. His main rival is Tom, who is eighth on 321 points and — look, we're not going to say he was at a festival, we're just going to say that when we tried to reach him for comment, the response we got back was enthusiastic, warm, and completely incoherent.

Down in the bottom third: Dave is eleventh on 300 points, still dreaming of his Culture Show segment — 'Trossy's Wafflecock' — in which he would hold forth on cultural nothingness with great conviction and a pint in hand, possibly accompanied by a single crash of the cymbals. Dan is seventh on 329, living off-grid, quietly brilliant, allied with Malik who is last on 280 and managing from Iceland, where he has been signing anyone who has ever worn a Manchester United or Portugal shirt. Portugal, you may have noticed, are out. Malik is aware. Malik is processing. And finally, Joe S — sixth on 338, universally beloved, a genuinely gifted footballer whose career was spent more in the physio room than on the pitch, and whose relationship with modern technology remains, by all accounts, a work in progress. He is fine. He is always fine. He is the sort of man who is fine.

The Final is coming. One match. Thirteen managers. Some of them ready, some of them injured, one of them still shouting his team name, and at least one algorithm somewhere in a lab trying to work out what year it is. We'll see you on the other side.

The Final is the last fixture standing — after that, the tournament is done, the scores are locked, and someone lifts the trophy. Joe A has the lead. Tristan has the look. Everyone else has hope, stubbornness, or at minimum a strong opinion about it. Don't go anywhere.
