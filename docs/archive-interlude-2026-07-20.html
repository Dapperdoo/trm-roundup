THE MORNING AFTER

Interlude Special
No football. Just thirteen people to mock.

There are no fixtures today. The World Cup has paused, the pitches are resting, and somewhere a groundsman is finally getting eight hours of sleep. Which means we have nothing to report — no goals, no blanks, no heroics, no disasters. Nothing except thirteen fantasy managers sitting in various states of anxiety, delusion and quiet denial. So pull up a chair. Tonight, the football takes the night off and we go to work.

Let's start at the top, because Joe A is sitting on 461 points and a one-man-crusade against the entire AI industry. First in the league, genuinely pleased about it, and spending his victory lap doing exactly what he does every other hour of every other day: pasting a prompt into some AI tool, watching it confidently promise him the earth, and then staring at the output like a man who has just been handed a parking ticket written in Esperanto. Joe A, mate — you're leading the league. Put the laptop down. The AI is not going to deliver. It never does. You know this. We all know this. The Guinness, at least, keeps its promises.

Breathing down Joe A's neck are Nick and Tristan, and honestly the tension between those two is nothing compared to the tension Nick generates on a football pitch. Nick, you are six foot something of pure reckless chaos. The shooting — launched skyward like a man trying to dislodge a cat from a very tall tree. The tackling — technically illegal in several jurisdictions. You don't slide in, you commit. Referees don't book you; they file incident reports. Tristan, meanwhile, is over there doing things properly — hand-gluing every single data point from the FIFA website into Jez's spreadsheet one number at a time, meticulous as a jeweller, furious as a man who has just been handed a parking ticket written in Esperanto. (He and Joe A should compare notes.)

Jeremy sits fifth on 360 points, and he built the spreadsheet that holds this entire operation together, which means he is simultaneously the most valuable person in the league and the one most entitled to look quietly unimpressed by everyone else in it. He does not use an algorithm. He does not use AI. He does not use a 'system'. He uses his brain, his taste, and forty years of accumulated football knowledge — and he is fine, thank you — which is exactly the expression he has been wearing since Lloydy's algorithm started firing bids for players who last kicked a ball in Qatar. Speaking of which.

Chris. Lloydy. The mad scientist of the group. The man who built a transfer algorithm and accidentally invented a time machine. Five bids — five! — for players from the 2022 World Cup. The bot wasn't scouting; it was holding a séance. It was trying to sign ghosts. It ground the entire league's transfer window to a halt while it rummaged through a four-year-old squad list like your nan going through a biscuit tin looking for a specific Bourbon. It did eventually land one valid bid — a player who scored 8 points, fair play — but that's like burning down a kitchen and being praised for the toast. Jake, for his part, has never been more vindicated in his life, and he would like everyone to know it.

Jake. Snacob's Ladder! Yes, that's him, shouting his own team name again, apropos of absolutely nothing, in what appears to be a dentist's waiting room — because Jake loves the dentist. Genuinely loves it. Goes voluntarily. Looks forward to it. The man who has injured himself on the way to the dentist, injured himself coming back from the dentist, and presumably once pulled something reaching for the appointment card — but he keeps going, beaming, because the dentist is a safe space. On the pitch, less safe. On the dancefloor, actively dangerous — currently holding a full tray of drinks and moving towards the music with the confidence of someone who has never once spilled anything, which is statistically extraordinary given his record. His Harry Kane budget-buster sits at the top of the squad like a very expensive ornament. And he still hasn't forgiven Jeremy for the transfer swap. Jeremy has, by all accounts, entirely moved on.

Sam — fourth, 361 points, and the man whose face is a complete emotional weather system in real time. Every goal, every blank, every offside flag: it's all there, live, on his face, before he's even processed it consciously. Sam once signed a year-long lease on a house because the viewing had its own bar, moved in on day one, and discovered there was no bathroom. He looked at those empty bar barrels for a long time. His brother Wigs — tenth, 319 points, counsellor by trade — presumably talked him through it. Wigs is the kind of person who makes everyone feel heard, which is a wonderful quality, and also means he has sat through more of Sam's theatrical football reactions than any human being should reasonably have to endure. Brotherly love, tested daily.

Dan is seventh, living off-grid, quietly brilliant, and almost certainly already knows something about this tournament that the rest of us won't figure out for another week. His ally Malik is managing from Iceland — thirteenth, yes, but doing so with tremendous conviction, having assembled a squad that could double as a Manchester United reunion tour with a Portuguese annexe. Malik signs anyone who has ever worn those shirts. It is a philosophy. It is perhaps not winning as a philosophy, but it is a philosophy. Dave, meanwhile, is eleventh and composing a poem about it — probably a good one, full of wordplay and cultural reference, destined for a segment called Trossy's Wafflecock on a television programme that has not yet agreed to have him. And Tom — eighth, 321 points — is somewhere. Genuinely unclear where. Very happy, by all accounts.

Joe S is sixth on 338 points, which is a solid return for a man who spent a significant portion of his professional football career horizontal. Joe S was one of the most talented players of his generation and the physio room got more of him than the pitch did. He is beloved by everyone in this league, baffled by most of the technology that runs it, and would almost certainly rather be at a party than reading this. Which is fair. Most of us would.

The World Cup final is on the horizon — one match left, one nation to be crowned, and thirteen managers with everything still to play for in the table. Joe A's lead is real but the chasing pack is close enough to make things interesting. Rest up. The football is coming back, and when it does, this bulletin will be waiting — assembled, as always, by a machine that has no idea what it's doing but is doing it anyway.
