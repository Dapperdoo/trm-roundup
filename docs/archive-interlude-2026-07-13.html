THE MORNING AFTER

Interlude Special
No Football. Just Thirteen People Who Deserve This.

There are no fixtures today. The World Cup pauses, the pitches rest, the VAR officials briefly consider their life choices — and that means, for one special edition, we turn the spotlight away from the football entirely and point it squarely at the thirteen individuals who have made this league the magnificent, chaotic, occasionally baffling institution it is. Pull up a chair. This is a roast. It's affectionate. Mostly.

Let's start at the top, because Joe A is sitting there on 399 points and he'd like you to know that his squad basically runs itself. What Joe actually does with his time is wage a one-man war against AI tools that refuse to do what they claim — a noble, doomed crusade conducted largely from a sofa, Guinness in hand, surrounded by the archaeological layers of a man who has never once thrown anything away. He will spend forty-five minutes getting a chatbot to format a spreadsheet, it will confidently produce something unusable, and he will start again. The irony that he is being chased by Tristan — a man who runs his entire fantasy operation by hand, squinting at the FIFA website and writing numbers down like it's 1987 — is not lost on anyone. Tristan is third, six points back, and closing. He doesn't need an algorithm. He needs a biro and a grudge, and he has both.

Speaking of grudges — Jake. Snacob's Ladder! There he is, eleventh, still seething. Jake carries two grievances at all times like a man who's packed for a very specific holiday. Grievance one: Jeremy's transfer correction from a couple of days ago, in which an injured player was quietly swapped for an uninjured one — every other manager shrugged and moved on, but Jake is still there, still holding the receipt, still asking to speak to a manager. Grievance two: whatever Lloydy is doing with AI. Jake doesn't know exactly what it is. He suspects it involves the office complex. He suspects it involves something that should not exist. He is probably wrong. He is not going to stop. In the meantime, Jake injured himself last week — not at training, not in a match, not even doing anything that could charitably be called physical activity. On his way to the dentist. Which, in fairness, is the one place Jake genuinely loves going. He speaks about his dental appointments with a warmth most people reserve for holidays or newborns. 'Snacob's Ladder!' he shouts, unprompted, at the waiting-room receptionist. She has learned to smile and nod.

Chris — Lloydy — is eighth on 289 and remains unrepentant about the algorithm. For those who missed it: his AI transfer system, in its most recent outing, fired five consecutive bids for players from the 2022 World Cup. Five. In a row. It wasn't confused — it was nostalgic. It was holding a séance. It eventually landed one valid bid, the player scored 8 points, and Lloydy has been dining out on that single data point ever since. Jake, who has been warning about this for months, is vindicated and furious simultaneously, which is his natural state. Jeremy, meanwhile — the professor, the man who built the very spreadsheet this league runs on — looks at all of this from fifth place with the quiet, withering patience of someone who could have built a working algorithm years ago and simply chose not to, because some things are better done by a human being who actually watched the football.

Joe S is sixth on 310, which is a remarkable achievement for a man who spent the better part of his playing career horizontal. Joe S was a genuinely gifted footballer — ask anyone — but the physio room knew him better than the pitch did. He'd turn up for pre-season, look incredible for a fortnight, and then something would go. He is now baffled by the technology required to run a fantasy team and is dealing with it in the way he deals with most things: charm, instinct, and hoping someone nearby knows the password. It's working. Six isn't bad.

Sam is second — second! — on 338, which is either a sign of genuine quality or proof that the universe rewards a man who once signed a year-long lease on a house because it had its own bar, only to discover on moving day that it had absolutely no bathroom. Sam is a performer, a cricket devotee, a Shakespeare man, a dancer, a beer enthusiast, and the brother of Wigs, who is ninth. The O'Shea brothers: one near the top, one in the middle, both counselling themselves through it in their own way. Wigs, for his part, is gregarious and gentle and witty and probably the most emotionally well-equipped person in this entire league to handle a mid-table finish. That's not an insult. That's a compliment. He's fine. He's processing it.

Nick is fourth on 328, which is excellent, and Nick would like you to know this. Nick is very tall and very loud and on a football pitch he is a reckless, clattering menace — the kind of player who goes in for a challenge and the referee reaches for a card before he's even made contact. His shooting is a separate issue entirely. The ball goes up. It keeps going. Somewhere, a seagull is nervous. His main rival is Tom, who is seventh on 305, and who — look, we're not going to say where Tom is right now, but the last confirmed sighting involved someone who sounded approximately like him, very happy, not entirely sure which continent he was on. His team is doing fine. Someone is presumably keeping an eye on it.

Down in the bottom three, we have Dan on 283, Malik on 276, and Dave on 274. Dan is smart, off-grid, and historically a very good fantasy manager — he and Malik are allies, which is a lovely arrangement between a man living in deliberate isolation and a quirky Icelander managing from somewhere even further away, who signs players on the sole criterion that they have at some point worn a Manchester United or Portugal shirt. Portugal, you will note, have been eliminated. Malik is taking this with the stoic dignity of a man from a country that has weathered worse. Dave, meanwhile, is at the bottom of the table and has been composing a poem about it. It has seventeen stanzas. It contains three puns. He is considering pitching the whole thing to The Culture Show under the working title 'Trossy's Wafflecock: A Meditation on Thirteenth Place.' Honestly? Commission it.

Football returns on Tuesday 14 July at 20:00 UK time — the quarter-finals, where nations fall and squads get thinner and the gap between first and thirteenth starts to feel very real indeed. Rest up. Rehydrate. Jake, please do not injure yourself between now and then. The dentist will still be there next week.

The next fixtures land Tuesday 14 July at 20:00 UK — quarter-final football, knockout stakes, and thirteen managers who have absolutely no excuses left. The Morning After will be back with numbers, recriminations, and whatever Jake has done to himself in the meantime.
