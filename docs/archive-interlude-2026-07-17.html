THE MORNING AFTER

Interlude Special
No football. Just thirteen people to embarrass.

There are no fixtures today. The World Cup semi-finals are almost upon us, four nations left standing, and the rest of the planet is just sitting in the gap between matches, staring at the wall. So we thought: why waste a perfectly good Tuesday doing nothing? Let's turn the spotlight away from the football and onto the thirteen people who have been shouting at it. Ladies and gentlemen — and Dave — welcome to the Morning After Interlude. We're going to work the room.

Let's start at the top, because someone has to. Joe A sits first in the league on 415 points, which is a genuinely impressive number, and he would like you to know that he is very focused on defending that lead. He is, in fact, so focused that he has spent the last three days trying to get an AI tool to build him a semi-final scouting report, and it has so far produced a seventeen-page document about a tournament that ended in 2019. He is on his fourth Guinness and his sixth browser tab. The lead is real. The method is chaos. Tristan, breathing down his neck in third on 358, has been doing it the old-fashioned way — manually combing the FIFA website, row by row, number by number, like a man who genuinely does not trust the internet to do anything right and has decided to do everything himself. He throws his hands up in disgust at least four times per session. At this point the disgust is load-bearing. It is holding the whole operation together.

Nick is also on 358, technically level with Tristan and occupying second on goal difference or some such tiebreaker, and Nick would like everyone to know this. Nick would like everyone to know most things, at considerable volume, from a considerable height. On the football pitch Nick is a reckless, clatter-first menace who treats a sliding tackle the way a wrecking ball treats a condemned building — enthusiastically, indiscriminately, and with very little regard for what's on the other side. Off it he is warm and generous and fun. On it he is a public health concern. His shots, meanwhile, continue to clear the crossbar by distances that would be impressive if he were attempting a throw-in.

Sam is fourth, which is remarkable given that Sam approaches fantasy football the way he approaches most things — with enormous enthusiasm, genuine wit, and the faint impression that he's also thinking about something else entirely. Possibly Shakespeare. Possibly cricket. Possibly whether the venue has a decent bar. His brother Wigs is tenth, which is the kind of gap that makes family dinners interesting. Wigs is a counsellor by trade and is therefore professionally equipped to process difficult emotions, which is handy, because being nine places behind your sibling in a thirteen-team league is exactly the kind of thing you'd normally pay someone to help you through. He is, at least, very pleasant about it.

Jeremy is fifth, and Jeremy is fine with fifth, because Jeremy is fine with most things in a quietly devastating way. He built the spreadsheet that the entire league runs off. He could, if he wanted, build an algorithm to pick his team. He has chosen not to. He looks at Chris's much-discussed AI transfer engine — the one that spent the better part of an afternoon firing bids at players from the 2022 World Cup, holding the entire league's window hostage while it rummaged through a squad list four years out of date — and Jeremy says nothing. He doesn't need to. The expression does it. Chris, for his part, is in ninth on 297 and remains absolutely committed to the idea that the algorithm just needs a bit of a tune-up. The algorithm, presumably, is currently trying to sign someone who has since retired and opened a restaurant.

Jake is eleventh on 289, and Jake is furious about the transfers situation — not the algorithm, not the standings, but Jeremy's honest administrative correction from a couple of days ago, which every other manager accepted immediately and moved on from, and which Jake has not. Jake cannot move on. Jake will not move on. Jake is still there, in the group chat, typing. In fairness to Jake, he has had a difficult week: he injured himself on the way to the dentist, which is the kind of sentence that only makes sense if you already know Jake. He is fine. He is always fine. He has the injury record of a man who pulls a hamstring opening the fridge, and the dental enthusiasm of someone who has never once had a bad check-up. 'SNACOB'S LADDER!' he announced to no one in particular this morning, apparently just to confirm that he and his team still exist. They do, Jake. They do.

Joe S is sixth on 326, which is a perfectly respectable position for a man who spent a significant portion of his actual football career horizontal on a treatment table. The physio room knew his name. The physio room had his mug. Joe S was, by all accounts, a genuinely brilliant footballer when he was upright, which was — let's be generous — about half the time. He has carried this energy into retirement with great style and absolutely no interest in learning how his phone works.

Tom is seventh on 307, and Tom is — we're told — around. Somewhere. His team is ticking over. Dan is eighth on 305, quietly doing his thing off-grid in a way that suggests he has thought more carefully about the semi-finals than anyone who actually has WiFi. Dan and Malik — thirteenth, bottom, 278 points, managing from Iceland, signing anyone who has ever pulled on a Manchester United or Portugal shirt — remain the league's most dependable alliance, which is sweet, and also a little alarming given that Portugal were eliminated some time ago and Malik's transfer philosophy has therefore just lost its entire southern European wing.

Dave is twelfth on 280, and Dave is a poet and a lecturer and a man who genuinely believes that a segment called 'Trossy's Wafflecock' on a BBC arts programme would constitute a meaningful contribution to public discourse. He plays the cymbals. Only on special occasions. We don't know what qualifies. We're not sure he does either. He is, however, very good at puns, and in a league this chaotic, that might yet be the most useful skill of all.

Four teams remain. The semi-finals are coming. Whoever is still alive in your squad is worth their weight in gold; whoever isn't has joined the long, proud list of eliminated nations that Lloydy's algorithm will probably try to sign in the next window. Get some rest, check your squads, and we'll see you when the football returns.
