THE MORNING AFTER

Interlude Special
No football today. The managers are the entertainment.

Right. There are no fixtures today. The World Cup has paused, the pitches are empty, and for once nobody is losing points in real time. Which means we have a rare and precious opportunity to do something this bulletin doesn't do nearly enough: stop talking about the football and start talking about the people who are absolutely ruining their weekends over it. Ladies and gentlemen — your thirteen managers. Pull up a chair. This won't take long. It'll just feel like it does.

Let's start at the top, because Joe A is sitting on 399 points and a league lead that is, frankly, starting to look slightly embarrassing for the other twelve. Shatner's Bassoon: first place, clear of the field, apparently thriving. You'd think this was a man with a system. A plan. A method. In reality, Joe spends the majority of his waking hours trying to get AI tools to do what they say on the tin — a crusade that, by all accounts, has never once fully succeeded. He is essentially fighting the same war as Lloydy, except Joe is fighting it with a Guinness in hand and the quiet, cheerful resignation of a man who has accepted the machine will always disappoint him. And yet: first place. Meanwhile, Tristan — big, Russian-raised, sweet-toothed, and not a man who suffers fools — is third on 333, glaring at the back of Joe's head from two rows behind and manually cross-referencing every single data point by hand from the FIFA website like a man who trusts absolutely no one. Which, honestly, in this league? Correct.

Second place belongs to Sam — 338 points, stage performer, cricket lover, devoted Shakespearean, and a man whose life decisions are, shall we say, bold. This is someone who once signed a year-long lease on a house because the viewing had its own bar. Moved in. Discovered there was no bathroom. Stood there in an empty room next to a row of barrels, doing the maths. Sam looked at that situation and apparently thought: 'I can work with this.' He's also the man who, after one particularly committed evening, fell over so comprehensively that both shoes left his feet simultaneously — as if his body had simply decided it was done and filed the paperwork. And yet he quotes Hamlet at you with complete sincerity the next morning. Brother of Wigs, who is ninth on 287, gentle, gregarious, and presumably the sibling who remembers to check whether a house has a bathroom before signing anything.

Fourth on 328 is Nick. Nick is very tall, very loud, and on a football pitch he is an absolute menace — a reckless, clattering, badly-mistimed tackler who leaves a trail of opponents wondering what just happened and whether it was intentional. (It was intentional.) Off the pitch he is perfectly lovely. On it he is a one-man insurance claim. His shooting, meanwhile, goes high, wide, and somehow still climbing — like a golf ball struck by someone who has never seen a golf course but has heard good things. His main rival is Tom, seventh on 305. Tom is a DJ. He plays one genre, and one genre only, at volumes that make the ground vibrate. We won't go into what happened to his last set of festival-goers, but eyewitness reports were largely incoherent, very happy, and smelled faintly of incense.

Fifth is Jeremy — the professor, 322 points, builder of the original league spreadsheet, a man of genuine brilliance who picks his team on instinct, taste and experience alone. He could build an algorithm. He has chosen not to. He looks at what Lloydy has done with one and files it quietly under 'evidence'. Jeremy is also a 5-a-side tackler of some notoriety — not reckless like Nick, just precise, slightly spiteful, and technically within the rules if you squint. Sixth is Joe S on 310, which is a remarkable return for a man who spent roughly half his actual playing career horizontal on a treatment table. Joe S was a genuinely gifted footballer — flair, vision, the lot — and injury took chunks of it away with almost comic regularity. He loved the lifestyle too, which may or may not have contributed. He is also, by all accounts, completely baffled by modern technology, which makes it all the more impressive that he's managed to operate a fantasy football team at all.

Now. Chris. Lloydy. Eighth on 289. Chris is the league's mad scientist — always moving, always building something, always with a scheme. In the most recent transfer window, his much-vaunted algorithm — lovingly constructed, proudly deployed — fired off five consecutive bids for players from the 2022 World Cup. Five. In a row. Players who were, at that point, either retired, unavailable, or simply not at this tournament. The entire league's transfer window ground to a halt while the machine held a séance for the class of four years ago. It eventually landed one valid bid. The player scored 8 points. Lloydy has chosen to focus on the 8. Jake — eleventh, 278 points, renewable energy manager, mushroom enthusiast, wind turbine devotee — has chosen to focus on everything else. Jake was vindicated. Jake knows he was vindicated. Jake will not be letting this go. 'SNACOB'S LADDER!' he shouts, apparently to no one in particular, and then goes back to glowering at Lloydy's general direction.

Jake, bless him, is also carrying a grudge about the recent transfer correction — in which Jeremy, having made an honest administrative error, quietly swapped out an injured player and replaced him with a fit one. Every other manager nodded and moved on. Jake has not moved on. Jake is still there, at the scene, in a forensic tent, taking measurements. It is worth noting that Jake injures himself at a rate that would make Joe S wince — the standout incident being the time he hurt himself on the way to the dentist. On the way there. He had not yet arrived. He loves the dentist, by the way. Absolutely loves it. Talks about it constantly. The clean teeth, the chair, the little mirror on a stick. He will dance his way to the appointment, tray of drinks in hand, sloshing them over everyone in a ten-foot radius, arrive injured, and come out beaming.

Down in the basement, we find Dave on 274 — last place, but spiritually unbothered. Dave is a lecturer, a poet, a man who loves a pun, a city break, a cigarette, and a pint. He still dreams of hosting his own segment on The Culture Show — 'Trossy's Wafflecock' — in which he would hold forth on cultural nothingness with total conviction and a glass of something decent. He plays the cymbals, but only on special occasions. The standings have not yet constituted a special occasion. Malik is twelfth on 276, managing from Iceland with characteristic quirk, signing anyone who has ever pulled on a Manchester United or Portugal shirt — which is, given that Portugal are now eliminated, an increasingly poignant strategy. His ally Dan is tenth on 283, living off-grid, doing things his own way, historically very good at this, and probably the most quietly dangerous man in the bottom half of the table.

One last thing worth noting: Portugal are out. Which means every Portugal player in this league — and Malik has several — is done. Gone. Finished. They have, in a sense, joined the exact cohort of players Lloydy's algorithm was already trying to sign. Welcome to the 2022 squad, lads. Lloydy's bot will be in touch.

The quarter-finals are upon us, and when the next fixtures land, thirteen managers will be back at their screens, refreshing frantically, pretending they're not nervous. Until then — no football, no points, no excuses. Just twelve people watching Joe A's lead and wondering how a man whose main hobby is arguing with chatbots got to 399.
