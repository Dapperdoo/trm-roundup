THE MORNING AFTER

Interlude Special
No football. Just thirteen people to mock.

Good morning. There are no fixtures today. The World Cup has paused for breath somewhere between the quarter-finals and the semi-finals, the pitches are empty, the VAR officials are presumably at a spa, and we have absolutely nothing to report. Which means we can do something far more valuable: turn the spotlight on the thirteen human beings who have been torturing themselves over this fantasy league for weeks, and ask — lovingly, forensically, without mercy — what exactly is going on with all of you?

Let's start at the top, because Joe A is sitting on 415 points and a 57-point lead, and he would like you to know that his AI tools are still not doing what they promised. Joe spends — and I want to be precise here — enormous amounts of his finite time on this earth trying to get AI software to deliver on its claims. It never quite does. He cajoles it, reprompts it, reads the documentation, reprompts it again, and eventually gets something that is almost but not entirely what he wanted. And yet, somehow, he is first. Imagine what he could do if the technology worked. Actually, don't — Tristan is already imagining it and it's making him furious. Tristan is third, 57 points back, and he has done everything right: he gleans every single number by hand from the FIFA website, cross-references it against Jez's spreadsheet, and throws his hands up in disgust at the result anyway. He is the most diligent man in this league. He is also losing. He would like a word.

Sandwiched between them at second is Nick, also on 358, separated from Tristan only by goal difference or alphabetical order or some other coin-flip that will one day be settled on a football pitch — probably one where Nick has already been sent off. Nick on a football pitch is a public health concern. Six-foot-something of reckless, badly-timed, occasionally deliberate chaos, flying into challenges like a man who has confused 'football match' with 'controlled demolition'. His shooting, meanwhile, sails so consistently over the crossbar that local councils in three counties have issued warnings to low-flying aircraft. His main rival is Tom, who is seventh and therefore not currently a rival in any meaningful sense, but Nick will not hear this.

Tom is seventh on 307. Tom has never won this league. He has been playing it for years, he is a perfectly capable fantasy manager, and he has never won it. At some point this becomes a philosophical question rather than a statistical one. We wish him well. We always wish him well. We will be wishing him well again next year.

Fourth is Sam, and Sam is proof that you can be a Shakespearean, cricket-loving, beer-drinking, music-obsessed stage performer with the most expressive face in professional entertainment and still be genuinely good at fantasy football. Sam once signed a year's lease on a house because the viewing had its own bar, moved in, and discovered there was no bathroom. He adapted. That is Sam's whole deal, really — he adapts, he performs, he finds a way. Currently finding a way to fourth place. His brother Wigs is tenth, which means that at family dinners Sam gets to be quietly smug and Wigs gets to be a counsellor about it, which is convenient given that he is one.

Fifth is Jeremy. The professor. The man who built the spreadsheet that underpins this entire operation — from scratch, in his own time, because he could. Jeremy picks his team on instinct, taste, and accumulated expertise, and he is fifth. He does not use an algorithm. He has never used an algorithm. He could build one before breakfast, and he has looked at what algorithms have done to this league and decided, quietly and without fanfare, that he was right not to bother. He says nothing. He doesn't need to.

What he's looking at, specifically, is Chris. Lloydy. Ninth. Chris built an algorithm for the transfer window. It malfunctioned. It fired off five consecutive bids for players from the 2022 World Cup — players who, in some cases, have since retired, aged out, or are simply no longer in a tournament that ended four years ago. Then it fluked one valid bid, the player scored 8 points, and Chris declared this a partial success. Jeremy did not declare anything. He just looked at Chris the way a master watchmaker looks at someone who has glued a sundial to a Rolex. And Jake — eleventh, 289 points, absolutely seething — Jake has been vindicated so thoroughly that he cannot stop saying so. 'SNACOB'S LADDER!' he shouts, to no one in particular, reminding himself of who he is and what he stands for. What he stands for, currently, is a grudge against Jeremy over a transfer correction from a couple of days ago that every other manager has moved on from, and a paranoid running theory about what Lloydy is secretly building with AI in his private office complex. Jake injured himself on the way to the dentist last month — on the way there, before he even got to the chair — and he is still going back for his next appointment because he genuinely loves it. 'Snacob's Ladder!' He loves the dentist. He is a complicated man.

Down at the bottom, Dave is twelfth on 280, dreaming of a Culture Show segment called Trossy's Wafflecock in which he would hold forth on cultural nothingness with great conviction and a pint. Dave plays the cymbals. Only on special occasions. We have not yet established what constitutes a special occasion for Dave, but we suspect it involves a city break, a good meal, a cigarette, and a pun so layered that it takes three days to fully unpack. Just below him is Malik, thirteenth, managing from Iceland with the serene confidence of a man whose transfer strategy — sign anyone who has ever worn a Manchester United or Portugal shirt — has been somewhat complicated by the fact that Portugal were eliminated. Malik's main ally is Dan, eighth, living off-grid, doing things his own way, historically excellent at this game. Dan and Malik against the world. The world is currently winning, but only just.

And then there is Joe S. Sixth. Back of the Van United. A man of genuine, luminous footballing talent whose actual playing career was spent largely horizontal on a treatment table while the physio shook their head slowly. He is beloved by everyone in this league, which is lucky, because if they didn't love him they might point out that he has spent more time injured than playing in almost every context in which football has been asked of him. He is baffled by technology, he loved the lifestyle, and he is sixth in the standings, which is frankly more than most people manage while spending half their life in a compression bandage.

So. No football today. But thirteen managers, fully accounted for, all of them alive, most of them upright, one of them probably already at the dentist. The semi-finals are coming. The stakes are real. Someone is going to win this thing — and someone is going to spend the next four years blaming an algorithm.

The semi-finals are on the horizon. Four nations left standing. Every eliminated country takes its players with it — and somewhere in Lloydy's lab, the algorithm is probably trying to sign them. Rest up, everyone. It's nearly time.
