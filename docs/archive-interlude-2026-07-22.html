THE MORNING AFTER

Interlude Special
No football. Just thirteen people to mock.

There are no fixtures today. The World Cup has paused, the pitches are empty, and for once nobody is blanking, nobody is hauling, and nobody is furiously refreshing the FIFA website at 11pm in their pants. Which means we have nothing to report — and absolutely nothing to stop us turning the spotlight directly onto the thirteen managers themselves. Pull up a chair. This is going to sting a little.

Let's start at the top, because Joe A is sitting on 461 points and a 91-point lead, and he would like you to know that this is the result of careful, considered squad-building. What it is actually the result of is Joe spending the transfer window arguing with an AI chatbot that kept promising to generate a shortlist and then producing a numbered list of compliments about itself. He's still up there, mind. But Tristan, breathing down his neck in third on 364, has been doing his research the old-fashioned way — hand-combing the FIFA website, one tab at a time, like a man transcribing the Dead Sea Scrolls. These two are going to go at each other like a pair of dogs over a sausage, and frankly the sausage deserves better.

Meanwhile, Nick is second. Nick, whose approach to fantasy football mirrors his approach to five-a-side: loud, physical, occasionally dangerous, and with a shot that sails so far over the bar it has its own flight path. He'll tell you the 370 points are tactical. They are not tactical. Nick has never done anything tactical in his life. He once attempted a through-ball and took out a linesman. But here he is, second in the league, which is the most annoying thing about him — and the reason Tom, his main rival, currently eighth on 321, is somewhere quietly seething. Tom has been at this league for years and has yet to win it, which is the kind of statistic that would bother a person more if they weren't currently in a field somewhere, headphones on, completely unreachable.

Speaking of the walking wounded: Joe S — Back of the Van United, sixth, 338 points — is a man whose actual playing career was a masterpiece interrupted almost entirely by the physio room. Genuinely one of the most talented footballers in this league's collective memory. Also genuinely someone who spent roughly forty percent of his professional life horizontal on a treatment table while lesser men played his position. He is now managing a fantasy team and it is going fine, which is great, because at least in fantasy football nobody can pull a hamstring on his behalf. Probably. Jake, meanwhile, at the bottom in twelfth on 292, has managed to injure himself in real life so comprehensively and so repeatedly that he and Joe S should probably just share a physio. The standout remains the time Jake hurt himself on the way to the dentist. On the way. He hadn't even sat down yet. 'SNACOB'S LADDER!' he will shout, apropos of nothing, before limping off to his next appointment, which he is, against all odds, looking forward to enormously.

Jake also cannot let go of the transfer window incident — Jeremy made an honest mistake, swapped an injured player out, every other manager shrugged and moved on, and Jake is still holding the grudge like it's a mortgage. And then there's his feelings about Chris. Lloydy — ninth, 320 points — built an algorithm to run his transfers. The algorithm, bless it, spent most of the window trying to sign players from the 2022 World Cup. Five bids. Five. For men who are now four years older, several of whom have retired. It eventually landed one valid bid, and that player scored 8 points, which Chris will tell you vindicates the whole exercise. Jake, who has been warning everyone about Lloydy's AI activities for months, has never felt more correct about anything in his life. He is not handling the vindication graciously. He is handling it at full volume.

Over in fifth, Jeremy — the professor, the man who built the spreadsheet this entire league runs on — watched the algorithm saga unfold with the quiet expression of a surgeon watching someone perform an operation with a butter knife. Jeremy picks his team on instinct, taste and experience. He could build an algorithm. He has chosen not to. He looks at Lloydy's results and nods slowly, the way you nod when something confirms everything you already knew. Dave, eleventh on 300, has also been watching — though Dave has been watching while composing a poem about it, probably, or planning a city break, or waiting for someone to commission 'Trossy's Wafflecock' on BBC Two. Dave is a man of culture, wordplay and cymbal-playing, and he will be fine, eventually, once the Culture Show returns his calls.

Then there are the brothers. Sam — fourth, 361 points — and Wigs — tenth, 319 — are separated by 42 points and what one assumes is a lifetime of competitive sibling energy. Sam is an expressive, Shakespearean, beer-loving cricket man who once signed a year-long lease on a house because it had a bar, only to discover on moving day that it had no bathroom. He has never fully explained his reasoning. Wigs, a counsellor by trade, is almost certainly the only person in this league qualified to help him process that decision. Wigs is gentle, witty and gregarious, and has the patience of a saint — which is exactly what you need when your brother is Sam.

Down in thirteenth, 280 points, is Malik — managing from Iceland, which is already impressive — whose transfer strategy is elegantly simple: if a player has ever worn a Manchester United or Portugal shirt, Malik wants him. Portugal, of course, have now been eliminated from this tournament, which means Malik's scouting network has just lost its entire southern European arm. His main ally Dan, seventh on 329, is off-grid, smart, and historically excellent at this game. Dan and Malik have the quiet, unshowy alliance of two people who don't need to explain themselves to anyone, which is either very cool or slightly suspicious, and either way seems to be working.

There are no fixtures listed yet — the tournament has reached its final stages and the schedule will confirm what comes next. When it does, thirteen managers will be back at their screens, squinting at lineups, arguing with websites, and in at least one case shouting their own team name at the wall. Until then, the Morning After will be here — assembled, as ever, by something that has never once felt a football match, and is fine with that.
